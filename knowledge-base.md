question: What is the difference between Swedish and deep tissue massage?
answer: Swedish massage uses long, gentle, flowing strokes with light to medium pressure. It is designed for relaxation, improved circulation, and stress relief. It is ideal for first-time clients or anyone wanting a calming, full-body experience. Deep tissue massage uses slower, more focused strokes and firm pressure to reach the deeper layers of muscle and connective tissue. It is specifically beneficial for chronic pain, muscle tightness, injury recovery, and postural issues. Deep tissue can be intense but should not be painful.
tags: [Swedish, deep tissue, massage, difference, technique]

question: What is the difference between a chemical peel and microdermabrasion?
answer: A chemical peel uses alpha or beta hydroxy acids (like glycolic or salicylic acid) to chemically dissolve the outer layer of dead skin cells. It is effective for uneven skin tone, acne, sun damage, and hyperpigmentation. Multiple strength levels are available from superficial to medium depth. Microdermabrasion is a non-invasive mechanical exfoliation using ultra-fine crystals or a diamond-tip wand to physically buff away dead skin cells. It smoothes texture, minimizes pores, and stimulates cell renewal with zero downtime. Both are 45-minute treatments. We can recommend which is best for your skin concern.

question: Are there conditions that would prevent me from getting a massage?
answer: Massage is safe for most people but is not recommended when you have an active fever or infection, open wounds or burns in the treatment area, recent surgery within 6 weeks unless cleared by a doctor, blood clots or deep vein thrombosis, uncontrolled high blood pressure, active cancer without oncologist clearance, or are in the first trimester of pregnancy. Always disclose relevant health conditions when booking. We are happy to work with your healthcare provider to design a safe treatment plan.

Some mild muscle soreness after a deep tissue massage is normal for 1–2 days, similar to post-workout soreness.
